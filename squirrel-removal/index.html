Signs You Might Need Professional Squirrel Removal

Squirrels can be cunning creatures, and sometimes the signs of their presence in your home are subtle initially. However, ignoring these signs can lead to significant and costly damage. Recognizing the early warning signals is crucial for prompt action and preventing widespread problems. If you notice any of the following, contacting a professional for wildlife control and removal services is highly recommended.

Unusual Noises in Attic or Walls

One of the most common indicators of a squirrel infestation is the presence of strange noises emanating from your attic, ceilings, or within your walls, particularly during the daytime. Unlike nocturnal rodents such as rats or mice, squirrels are most active during the morning and afternoon. You might hear scratching, scurrying, gnawing, or even chattering sounds. These noises often signify that squirrels are building nests, moving around, or chewing on structural components. If you hear persistent noises, it's a strong sign that you likely have unwanted furry residents.

Visible Damage to Property

Squirrels are notorious for their persistent gnawing habits. They need to continuously chew to keep their teeth from overgrowing. This instinct often leads them to damage various parts of your property. Look for visible signs of damage, such as chewed wood around eaves, soffits, fascia boards, or trim. They might also damage vents, screens, or even shingles on your roof. This damage can compromise the structural integrity of your home and create new entry points for other pests. You might notice holes gnawed into siding or around windows.

Chewed Electrical Wires or Insulation

One of the most serious risks associated with squirrel infestations is the potential for them to chew on electrical wiring. This is incredibly dangerous and can create fire hazards. Attic spaces are often filled with wiring and insulation, making them attractive targets for squirrels looking for nesting material and a place to chew. If you suspect squirrels in your attic, it's essential to have a professional assess the situation to check for damaged wiring. Beyond electrical concerns, they can also shred and contaminate insulation, reducing its effectiveness and creating an unsanitary environment.

Presence of Droppings or Urine

Squirrel droppings and urine are clear indicators of an infestation. Squirrel droppings are typically small, pellet-shaped, and scattered in areas where they are active. Over time, an accumulation of droppings and urine can create foul odors, stain surfaces, and pose health risks due to the presence of bacteria and pathogens. Noticing these signs in your attic, garage, or even inside your home warrants immediate attention from a pest control professional.

Finding Nests

Squirrels build nests, often called dreys, in secluded and protected areas. In human structures, attics are common nesting sites. These nests are typically made from a variety of materials, including leaves, twigs, insulation, and shredded fabric. Discovering a squirrel nest in your attic or elsewhere on your property confirms an active infestation and likely indicates the presence of adult squirrels and potentially their young. Removing nests and the animals requires a humane and strategic approach.

Sightings of Squirrels Entering or Exiting the Home

The most obvious sign is witnessing squirrels entering or exiting your home, particularly through openings in the roofline, chimneys, or walls. Observing this behavior confirms that your home is being used as shelter or a nesting site. Pay attention to where they are going and coming from, as this can help identify entry points. This is a clear indication that professional intervention is needed to prevent them from continuing to use your home as their personal sanctuary.

Damaged or Flattened Insulation

Beyond chewing, squirrels will often move and flatten insulation material to create comfortable nesting areas or pathways within your attic. If you venture into your attic and notice sections of insulation that are compressed, pulled apart, or appear disturbed, it could be a sign of squirrel activity. This can significantly reduce the insulating properties of your home and lead to higher energy bills.

Strong, Lingering Musty Odors

An unpleasant, musty odor in your home, particularly in upper levels or enclosed spaces like the attic, can be a result of squirrel presence. This odor comes from their urine, droppings, and nesting materials. The smell can also be a sign of other wildlife infestations, such as raccoons or rodents. Investigating the source of persistent odors is important for maintaining a healthy indoor environment.

Accumulation of Food Stashes (Nuts, Seeds)

Squirrels are known for hoarding food. They might stash nuts, seeds, and other edible items in various locations within your home, including attics and wall voids. Discovering piles of nuts or other food items in unexpected places is a strong indicator that squirrels are using your home for storage as well as shelter. This food hoarding can attract other pests and create unsanitary conditions.

Increased Squirrel Activity Around Your Property

While seeing squirrels in your yard is normal, an unusual increase in their activity around your home's perimeter might suggest they are exploring potential entry points or have already found access. If you observe squirrels constantly climbing on your roof, scampering up walls, or investigating vulnerable areas of your home, it's wise to be proactive and have a professional assessment done.

Understanding Squirrel Behavior and Biology

To effectively manage squirrel infestations, it's helpful to understand a bit about their behavior and biology. Squirrels are members of the rodent family Sciuridae and are known for their agility, intelligence, and strong gnawing instincts. In North America, the most common species encountered in urban and suburban areas is the eastern gray squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis), although you might also encounter red squirrels (Tamiasciurus hudsonicus) or chipmunks (Tamias striatus), which are smaller but can still cause issues.

Life Cycle and Reproduction

Squirrels typically have two breeding seasons per year, one in late winter and another in summer. Females give birth to litters of usually 2-4 young after a gestation period of around 44 days. The young are born blind and helpless and remain in the nest for several weeks. Understanding the breeding cycles is important because removing mother squirrels without addressing the young humanely is not ideal. Our technicians are trained to identify if young squirrels are present and adjust the removal strategy accordingly to ensure humane treatment of the entire family group.

Diet and Foraging

Squirrels are primarily herbivores, feeding on a variety of plant matter including nuts, seeds, fruits, berries, and buds. They also occasionally eat insects, eggs, or even fungi. Their foraging behavior often brings them into conflict with homeowners, as they can raid gardens, bird feeders, and fruit trees. They are known for burying nuts and seeds as a food reserve, a behavior called caching. In urban environments, they become opportunistic feeders, readily adapting to available food sources, including garbage.

Nesting Habits

Squirrels build nests, known as dreys, for shelter and raising their young. In natural environments, these nests are often constructed in tree cavities or high up in tree branches using leaves, twigs, and other plant materials. However, in human environments, they readily adapt to using buildings as nesting sites. Attics, chimneys, and even wall voids can become attractive locations due to the warmth and protection they offer from predators and the elements. These urban nests are often made from insulation, shredded paper, and other readily available materials. The discovery of a drey within your home is a strong indicator of an ongoing infestation and the potential presence of a squirrel family.

Adaptability and Problem Solving

Squirrels are highly adaptable and intelligent animals. This intelligence allows them to solve problems, which is why they can be so persistent in their attempts to access homes and find food. They learn from experience and can quickly figure out how to overcome obstacles like fences or seemingly secure screens. Their ability to remember food locations contributes to their success in urban environments. This adaptability is one of the reasons why professional intervention is often necessary to address squirrel problems effectively.

Gnawing Behavior

As rodents, squirrels' incisor teeth grow continuously throughout their lives. To keep these teeth at a manageable length, they have a strong instinct to gnaw on hard surfaces. Unfortunately, this instinct can extend to the structural components of your home, including wood, wiring, and plumbing. This gnawing can cause significant damage, compromise the integrity of your home, and create fire hazards when electrical wires are involved. Understanding this behavior highlights the importance of addressing squirrel infestations promptly to prevent costly repairs.

Why Prevention is Key After Squirrel Removal

The Risks of Ignoring a Squirrel Problem

It can be tempting to hope a squirrel problem will simply go away on its own, but the reality is that ignoring these infestations can lead to a cascade of costly and potentially dangerous issues. Squirrels, while seemingly harmless from a distance, can wreak significant havoc within a home if left unchecked. Understanding these risks underscores the importance of prompt professional intervention.

Structural Damage

Squirrels are relentless gnawers, and this instinct can cause substantial structural damage to your home. They can chew through wood beams, joists, siding, and trim. This damage can weaken structural components and require expensive repairs. They can also damage vents, fascia boards, and soffits, creating openings that allow in moisture, leading to rot, mold growth, and attracting other pests like insects. Ignoring this type of damage compromises the long-term integrity of your property.

Electrical Hazards and Fire Risk

One of the most concerning risks of squirrel infestations is their tendency to chew on electrical wiring. Wires in attics, walls, and crawl spaces are particularly vulnerable. When squirrels strip the insulation from wires, it exposes the live conductors, creating a serious fire hazard. Damaged wiring can also lead to power outages or malfunctions in your home's electrical system. This risk alone is a compelling reason to address a squirrel problem immediately.

Damage to Insulation

Squirrels frequently use insulation as nesting material, tearing it apart and compacting it. This not only reduces the effectiveness of your insulation, leading to higher energy bills, but also contaminates it with urine and droppings. Damaged insulation can create cold spots in your home and may need to be fully replaced, which is a significant and costly undertaking. The presence of squirrel waste within the insulation also creates an unsanitary environment.

Contamination and Health Risks

Squirrel droppings and urine can accumulate over time, creating an unpleasant odor and posing health risks. Squirrel waste can carry various pathogens and parasites, including leptospirosis, salmonella, and roundworm (Baylisascaris procyonis), which can be harmful to humans and pets. Disturbing nests or coming into contact with contaminated areas without proper protection can expose you to these risks. Professional cleanup and sanitization are often necessary after squirrel removal.

Attraction of Other Pests

A squirrel infestation can inadvertently attract other pests to your home. Squirrel nests can harbor fleas, ticks, mites, and other parasites that can then spread throughout your home and infest your pets or family members. The food caches squirrels create can also attract insects and rodents like mice or rats looking for an easy meal. A single pest problem can quickly escalate into multiple infestations.

Spread of Disease to Pets

Squirrels can carry diseases and parasites that can be transmitted to your pets. Fleas and ticks residing in squirrel nests can easily jump onto your dog or cat, leading to discomfort and potential health issues for your furry companions. Diseases like leptospirosis can also be transmitted to pets through contact with contaminated urine or water sources.

Disturbance and Noise

While not as destructive as some of the other risks, the constant noise of squirrels scurrying, scratching, and gnawing in your walls or attic can be incredibly disruptive to your daily life and sleep. The sounds can be unsettling and create stress and anxiety for homeowners. Living with the constant reminder of wildlife in your home is an unpleasant experience that you shouldn't have to endure.
